## Executive Summary

This is a detailed conversation about Sam Parr's journey building Hampton, a peer group for CEOs that reached millions in revenue. The discussion reveals the "idea maze" - all the paths Sam considered but rejected before finding the right business model, target market, and brand positioning. Key insights include:

- **5 failed target markets** before finding product-market fit with CEO founders
- **The research process:** Conducted publicly on podcast with 50-70+ customer interviews
- **Branding investment:** $15,000 for branding agency with clear creative brief
- **Quality over growth:** Rejecting revenue to maintain community quality
- **Founder fit matters:** Building for an audience you understand intimately

## What is Hampton? The Business Model

**[04:45 - 05:44]**

### The Final Winning Idea: CEO Peer Groups

**Three Core Components:**

#### 1. Small Group Meetings (8-person "therapy groups")
- Similar size or types of companies
- Takes ~6 months to really gel and become awesome
- Members make presentations each meeting:
  - "Here's my portfolio, my net worth - am I doing something right or wrong?"
  - "Here's how my company works - what am I doing wrong/right?"
- Like a therapy group for business

#### 2. Digital Community
- Online forum for posting questions
- Both personal and business topics
- Examples: "I'm trying to hire a nanny - best agencies in the Northeast?"
- Peer advice and recommendations

#### 3. Events
- **200 events per year** at time of recording
- Includes retreats and various gatherings
- Community building and networking

### Inspiration:
Highly inspired by existing companies in the space:
- **Chief** (for women executives)
- **YPO** (Young Presidents' Organization)
- **Vistage** (CEO peer advisory)

### Results:
- Reached **millions of dollars in revenue** very early
- Did NOT use podcast audience initially
- Launched mostly privately
- Built it the right way from the start

## What They Said No To

**[14:12 - 17:18]**

### Big Decision #1: Watching Every Interview

**The Situation:**
- Hampton receives **~100 applications per day**
- They accept only a fraction
- To get into Hampton, you must interview

**What They Said NO To: Delegating This**
- Sam and partner Joe watch **100% of interviews**
- They are the **only people** who click approve/deny
- "That is our job - to watch 100% of the interviews"

**The Cost:**
- Turning down huge amounts of revenue
- "It fucking kills me. I'm adding up the numbers."

**Comparison: Chief.com**
- Grew to **$100M revenue in 4-5 years**
- How? They **let in 100% of people** who apply and interview
- Hampton doesn't do this

**The Temptation:**
- "I see all this missed revenue. Oh my God."
- Mental exercise: "What's the charcoal I could make from these people we've rejected?"
  - Reference: Kingsford charcoal invented because Ford had extra wood from furnaces

### Big Decision #2: Staying Focused

**What They Said NO To:**
- Creating products for rejected applicants
- Expanding to other demographics
- Multiple product lines

**What They Said YES To:**
"We're only focusing on CEOs of this demographic, of this type of business. And we're only going to do that until we say otherwise. And there's no time in the near future that we intend to say otherwise."

## Advice and Lessons Learned

**[16:18 - 20:16]**

### Advice Sam Received (and is implementing)

#### 1. Showcase the Curation Process
**[16:25 - 17:01]**

**The Problem:** Hampton's website doesn't clearly show how selective they are

**The Solution:**
- Show that you say no to many people
- Highlight how heavy the curation is
- "The founders watch every single interview"
- "They accept less than 1% of people"
- Have a counter showing:
  - How many people they've said no to
  - How much revenue they've turned down
- Message: "We put our money where our mouth is - we turned down this much revenue to keep quality high"

**Why It Works:** Makes people want to join MORE because it proves community quality

#### 2. Balance Elite with Cheeky
**[17:04 - 17:11]**

"You got the elite part right, now we have to make it cheeky"
- "I just went too elite douchey"
- Need to work on the other part of the brand promise

#### 3. Use the Founding Story
**[17:14 - 20:16]**

**Sam's Mistake:** Wanted to make it NOT "the Sam Parr business"
- "I think I screwed up. I think I should have leaned into that."

**The Strategy:**
- Founder CAN be the face for a period of time, then phase it out
- Did this successfully with The Hustle

**When Founder as Face DOESN'T Work:**
- When founder has to deliver the service
- Example: Law firm, marketing agency
- "I want THAT guy on my account"

**Why It DOES Work for Hampton:**
- Sam doesn't deliver the actual service
- He's not the account manager
- The peer group is the service

**The True Founding Story:**

The origin of Hampton IS Sam's own experience:
- Created peer group in San Francisco when running companies
- "We didn't know shit"
- Out of that group came:
  - A **billion dollar company**
  - A **$100 million company**
  - A **$30 million company**
  - Two failures
  - Best man at Sam's wedding
  - Friend who became investor
  - **This podcast** (My First Million)

**The Peer Group Process in Action:**
- "I'm going to do The Hustle - it's an events business"
- Next month: "No, it's not events, it's a blog business"
- Feedback from peer group: "You're showing up to a knife fight with a knife. You need a magic fucking wand."
- Sam's realization: "I'm not trying to build a billion dollar company. I'm trying to build a company that makes me X millions of dollars. That would change my life and be fucking awesome."

**The Value:** First-hand experience of how peer groups helped build successful businesses

## Additional Insights

### On Branding Agencies
**[20:16 - 21:28]**

**The Trade-off:**
- Feels indulgent when you have zero revenue
- "Now I'm going to take a break to do mood boarding and talk about how the brand needs to feel?"
- Internal voice: "Shut up, you idiot. Go build your business."

**Smart Approach (Milk Road Strategy):**
- Wanted to do brand right away
- Made it a reward: "When we get to 100,000 subscribers, we'll do the brand"
- "We can always rebrand and polish it up"

**When It's Worth It:**
- If confident about the idea
- It's a luxury item, not necessity
- **Exception: E-commerce brands** - "You've got to get your brand right"

### On Naming Agencies
**[21:28 - 25:56]**

**Sam's Experience:** Paid **$25,000** for naming agency

**What He Learned:**

#### The Good:
1. **The People Are Awesome**
   - Someone drawn to naming is super interesting
   - Actually should hire them away: "You're gonna run our whole brand. Come in-house."

2. **The Process Was Valuable**
   - Three people came in (maybe too many)
   - Asked deep questions: "What IS it?" (not just surface description)
   - Most pressure Sam ever felt in a pitch
   - Needed to capture the essence, not corporate BS

3. **The Creative Exercise** (MOST VALUABLE)
   - Guy moved table aside
   - Sat on floor with whiteboard
   - Wrote long sentences (not names - paragraphs!)
   - **First-person venting as the customer**

   Example (for dating app):
   > "I don't download a dating app. I download dating apps. All of them. Every single one. It's embarrassing. I've tried them all, use them all, hate them all. But I use them. I hate to use them. It's mostly me sending messages that aren't getting replies. The next message I send, I know probably won't get a reply. That's a pretty hopeless feeling. At least if I go to a girl at a bar, she rejects me to my face. Online? Cowards. These women are cowards."

   - Scratching stuff out: "No, that's not really true"
   - Like slam poetry
   - Getting to FEELING of problem and solution
   - Not in business plan format

   **Sam:** "I paid a very expensive $25K to learn that one process, which is actually very useful as a copywriter to get to the essence of what you're trying to do."

#### The Bad:
- Did exercises with flashcards
  - "Branson, Elon Musk, Einstein - pick two that fit"
  - "Corvette, Rolex, Louis Vuitton - pick two"
- Sam: "What is this, a personality quiz?"
- Hated the whole process
- **The name ended up sucking**
- Sam doesn't even remember what it was
- App failed by the time they delivered the name

**Conclusion:** Still thinks it's worth it, despite the high cost

## Key Takeaways

**[25:56 - 27:10]**

### 1. Do Your Research in Public
- Sam had guests on podcast who ran similar businesses
- Got the blueprint directly from them
- Was transparent about his intentions
- Created public paper trail

### 2. Expect Multiple False Starts (5 Failed Target Markets)
**The Process:**
1. Had an idea that sounded good in theory
2. Tried to contact potential customers
3. Asked: "Do you want this?"
4. Got punched in the face
5. Repeated 5 times

**The Pattern:**
- Ideas that looked great on paper failed in reality
- Some had no founder fit (minority entrepreneurs)
- Some he couldn't reach (Fortune 500 CEOs)
- Some he hated doing (Airbnb, trucking)
- Settled on what was probably the obvious idea (CEO founders)

### 3. Naming Can Be Simple
- Used streets from his neighborhood
- Asked only 2-3 people for feedback
- Went with gut feeling
- Hampton just "had a good sound to it"

### 4. Branding Brief Is Critical
**Garbage In, Garbage Out:**
- What you give the agency matters more than the agency itself
- Sam created specific document with:
  - One-line positioning: "Elite but cheeky"
  - Visual references (ads from 70s, Rolex, GQ)
  - Color palette (British racing green)
  - Sample copy he wanted
- This direction led to good output

### 5. Founder Story Is Powerful
- Be willing to be the face initially
- You can phase out later
- Only works if you're not delivering the service yourself
- Sam's peer group experience IS the founding story
- Shows proof of concept and genuine understanding

### 6. Quality Over Growth
- Watching every interview personally
- Turning down revenue to maintain standards
- Staying focused on one demographic
- Resisting temptation to expand too soon

### 7. Founder Fit Matters Most
- Build for audience you understand
- Build where you have reputation
- Build where you know the pain points
- Build where you know how to reach people
- "It was so much easier. It was way easier."
